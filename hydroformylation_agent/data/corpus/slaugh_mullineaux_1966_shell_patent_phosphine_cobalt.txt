SOURCE: Slaugh, L. H.; Mullineaux, R. D. "Hydroformylation of Olefins." U.S. Patent 3,239,566. Shell Oil Company, 1966.

SUMMARY:
This foundational Shell patent by Slaugh and Mullineaux is the first disclosure of phosphine-modified cobalt hydroformylation catalysis, specifically HCo(CO)3(PR3) with trialkylphosphines (predominantly tri-n-butylphosphine, Bu3P). It established that adding a sigma-donating, sterically bulky alkylphosphine to the cobalt hydridocarbonyl center: (a) dramatically stabilizes the catalyst against decomposition to cobalt metal, allowing operation at substantially lower CO partial pressures than HCo(CO)4; (b) increases L:B selectivity for linear aldehyde from approximately 3–4:1 (unmodified Co) to 7–9:1; and (c) increases hydrogenation activity, leading to direct aldehyde-to-alcohol conversion as a notable side reaction. This patent is the historical foundation of all phosphine-modified cobalt hydroformylation, including the cationic Co(II) bisphosphine systems used in this project.

KEY FINDINGS:
1. HCo(CO)3(PR3) with trialkylphosphines (Bu3P, Pr3P, Et3P) is a stable, active hydroformylation catalyst at 4–8 MPa (40–80 bar) total syngas pressure — significantly lower than the 20–30 MPa (200–300 bar) required for unmodified HCo(CO)4.
2. L:B ratios of 7–9:1 were achieved for terminal olefins (1-alkenes C4 to C12) using Bu3P-modified Co at 150–180°C — approximately double the L:B of unmodified HCo(CO)4 under comparable conditions.
3. The phosphine ligand increases electron density on Co through sigma-donation, which strengthens the Co–CO pi-backbonding. This stabilizes the catalyst against CO loss and decomposition to Co metal, enabling lower-pressure operation.
4. As a direct consequence of increased electron density, the catalyst also displays significantly enhanced hydrogenation activity: aldehydes are readily reduced to primary alcohols. This is desirable for the Shell higher olefin process (detergent alcohol production) but undesirable when the target is the aldehyde.
5. Higher temperatures (160–190°C) are required compared to Rh systems because the stronger Co–CO bond requires more thermal energy for the CO dissociation step that initiates olefin insertion.
6. Alkene isomerization is active: internal olefins can isomerize to terminal positions before hydroformylation, which is exploited in the Shell higher olefin process (SHOP) but complicates regioselectivity.
7. Trialkylphosphines (Bu3P) give better results than arylphosphines (PPh3) in the Shell process because the alkyl groups are stronger sigma-donors, providing greater Co–CO stabilization.

MECHANISTIC INSIGHT:
The phosphine ligand in HCo(CO)3(PR3) occupies one of the four coordination sites of HCo(CO)4, replacing one CO. The electron donation from the phosphine increases electron density at Co, which: (1) strengthens the remaining three Co–CO bonds via increased pi-backbonding, stabilizing the complex against CO loss and decomposition; (2) slows catalysis by making CO dissociation (required for olefin coordination) harder, necessitating higher temperature; (3) improves linear selectivity by increasing steric bulk around Co, disfavoring the branched olefin insertion pathway. The hydrogenation activity enhancement arises because the more electron-rich Co–H bond is a better hydride donor, facilitating aldehyde reduction.

RELEVANCE TO CATIONIC Co(II) BISPHOSPHINE AND TRIPHOS SYSTEMS:
The cationic Co(II) bisphosphine approach (Hood et al. 2020) was specifically designed to solve the problems of the Slaugh-Mullineaux system. By making the Co center cationic (charge +1), the electron density at Co is reduced compared to neutral HCo(CO)3(PR3), which: (a) weakens Co–CO bonds, re-enabling CO dissociation at lower temperatures and pressures; (b) reduces hydrogenation activity (less electron-rich Co–H is a poorer hydride donor, suppressing alcohol formation); (c) maintains improved selectivity from the chelating bisphosphine steric effect. The chelating bisphosphine also prevents the phosphine dissociation problems that plagued the monodentate Slaugh-Mullineaux catalyst at industrial temperatures.

PRACTICAL RECOMMENDATIONS FOR OPTIMIZATION (translated to BDP-2 and Triphos systems):
- The Slaugh-Mullineaux system confirms that phosphine ligand sigma-donation is the primary handle for catalyst stability vs. activity tradeoff. Cationic Co(II) bisphosphine systems escape this tradeoff by using charge to modulate electron density independently of ligand donor strength.
- Higher temperature was necessary in the Slaugh-Mullineaux system to overcome slow CO dissociation — the same is true for the cationic Co systems, but the activation temperature is lower (140–160°C vs. 160–190°C) due to reduced Co–CO bond strength.
- L:B of 7–9:1 was achieved by the Slaugh-Mullineaux monophosphine Co system. The cationic bisphosphine system of this project currently achieves L:B of 0.9–2.2 in seed data at 160°C — the target is to exceed the Slaugh-Mullineaux benchmark through condition optimization.
- Alcohol formation (hydrogenation side reaction) was a defining feature of the Slaugh-Mullineaux catalyst. The cationic Co(II) systems should show less alcohol formation due to reduced Co electron density, but should be monitored, especially at high temperature and H2-rich conditions.
- Pressure recommendation from this patent: 40–80 bar is effective for phosphine-modified Co. The seed data at 52 bar falls within this validated range.
