SOURCE: Aubry, D. A.; Bridges, N. N.; Ezell, K.; Stanley, G. G. "Polar Phase Hydroformylation: The Dramatic Effect of Water on Mono- and Dirhodium Catalysts." Journal of the American Chemical Society 2003, 125, 11180–11181. DOI: 10.1021/ja035926w

SUMMARY:
This communication from the Stanley group at LSU reports that adding 30% water (by volume) to acetone as solvent produces dramatic rate enhancements (30–265%) for the hydroformylation of 1-hexene with a range of Rh phosphine catalysts, including the dirhodium tetraphosphine catalyst and monometallic Rh/PPh3, Rh/Bisbi, Rh/Naphos, and Rh/Xantphos systems. Though Rh-based, this is directly relevant to understanding solvent polarity effects on hydroformylation activity and selectivity, especially for the Co bisphosphine system where tetraglyme (a polar, coordinating solvent) is the best performer.

KEY FINDINGS:
1. Adding 30% water to acetone gave 30–115% rate enhancement for monometallic Rh catalysts (PPh3, Bisbi, Naphos, Xantphos) for 1-hexene hydroformylation at 90°C, 6.2 bar, 1:1 H2:CO.
2. The dirhodium tetraphosphine catalyst [rac-Rh2(et,ph-P4)]2+ showed a 265% rate increase with 30% water in acetone, with L:B ratio of 33:1 and essentially no isomerization (<0.2%).
3. In pure acetone, the dirhodium catalyst gave L:B = 25:1 and initial TOF = 20 min-1 vs. L:B = 33:1 and TOF = 73 min-1 with 30% water — showing that polar solvent environment improves both activity and selectivity simultaneously.
4. Water-sensitive phosphite ligands (e.g., Billig bisphosphite from Union Carbide) cannot be used in aqueous systems due to hydrolysis.
5. The effect is attributed to increased polarity stabilizing the cationic or polar transition states in hydroformylation.

MECHANISTIC INSIGHT:
Polar solvents (water, acetone/water mixtures, tetraglyme) stabilize cationic or charge-separated transition states in the catalytic cycle, lowering the activation barrier for rate-determining steps. For cationic Co(II) bisphosphine systems, where the metal center carries a permanent positive charge, this effect should be pronounced — polar solvents stabilize the charged catalyst resting state and may lower the barrier for CO dissociation (a likely rate-limiting step). This provides mechanistic justification for why tetraglyme dramatically outperforms toluene, THF, and anisole in the seed data for both BDP-2 and Triphos catalysts.

PRACTICAL RECOMMENDATIONS FOR OPTIMIZATION:
- Polar, coordinating solvents are strongly preferred for cationic Co(II) bisphosphine hydroformylation — tetraglyme is the current best solvent based on experimental data.
- Increased solvent polarity generally improves both activity and selectivity for ionic/cationic transition-metal hydroformylation catalysts.
- Non-coordinating non-polar solvents (toluene) are strongly disfavored for the Co bisphosphine system — this is confirmed by near-zero conversion in toluene in the Triphos seed data.
- If switching solvents, prioritize polar, Lewis-basic solvents: tetraglyme > anisole > THF >> toluene, based on experimental data in this project.
- Water addition to polar solvents might further enhance activity for Co bisphosphine systems, though hydrolysis sensitivity of the precatalyst should be checked first.

SLAUGH AND MULLINEAUX PATENT NOTE (supplementary):
Slaugh, L. H.; Mullineaux, R. D. U.S. Patent 3,239,566 (Shell Oil Company), 1966.
This foundational patent established that HCo(CO)3(PR3) with trialkylphosphines (e.g., Bu3P) operates at lower pressure (4–8 MPa = 40–80 bar) and gives higher L:B ratios than unmodified HCo(CO)4, with direct hydrogenation of aldehyde to alcohol as a side reaction. The electron-donating alkylphosphine stabilizes the Co center via increased sigma-donation, reducing CO partial pressure requirements. This is the historical foundation for all phosphine-modified Co hydroformylation catalysis, including the cationic Co(II) bisphosphine system.
